# Discovery Call Script

## The Complete Framework for Qualifying Prospects and Winning Projects

**Part of The Client Machine: The AI-Powered Client Acquisition System for Freelancers**

---

## How to Use This Script

This is a conversation framework, not a word-for-word script. Your goal on a discovery call is to:

1. **Understand the prospect's situation** deeply enough to know if you can help.
2. **Uncover the real pain** behind the stated problem.
3. **Quantify the cost** of not solving the problem.
4. **Qualify them** on budget, timeline, and decision-making authority.
5. **Establish yourself as the expert** through the quality of your questions.

**Key principles:**
- Listen 70% of the time, talk 30%.
- Never pitch your solution until you fully understand their problem.
- Take detailed notes — you will need them for the proposal.
- Record the call (with permission) so you can reference their exact words.
- The best question you can ask is always "Tell me more about that."

**Estimated call duration:** 30-45 minutes.

---

## Pre-Call Checklist

Complete these items before every discovery call. Preparation is what separates freelancers who win projects from those who waste calls.

- [ ] **Research the company.** Visit their website, read their About page, understand what they sell and who they sell to. Check their blog, social media, and any recent press coverage. Spend at least 10 minutes.
- [ ] **Research the contact.** Look up the person you're speaking with on LinkedIn. Understand their role, how long they've been at the company, and any content they've published or shared recently.
- [ ] **Identify 2-3 observations.** Based on your research, note 2-3 specific things about their business that relate to your service. These will become conversation starters and demonstrate you've done your homework.
- [ ] **Prepare your case studies.** Have 2-3 relevant past projects ready to reference if the conversation calls for it. Know the specific results and metrics.
- [ ] **Set up your note-taking system.** Open a document with the section headers from this script so you can fill in notes during the call. Alternatively, set up a recording tool (Otter.ai, Fathom, Grain, or your phone's voice recorder) and ask for permission to record at the start of the call.

## Part 1: Opening (2-3 minutes)

**Goal:** Build rapport, set expectations for the call, and establish yourself as a professional who runs a structured process.

---

**Start with a warm greeting and small talk (30-60 seconds max):**

> "Hi [NAME], great to meet you. Thanks for taking the time today."

[If you have a personal connection point from your research, use it here — e.g., "I saw you posted about [TOPIC] on LinkedIn last week — really interesting perspective." Keep it to one sentence. Do not force small talk if there's no natural connection point.]

---

**Set the agenda (this is critical — it puts you in control of the call):**

> "Before we dive in, let me share how I usually structure these calls so we make the best use of the time.
>
> I'd love to spend the first part understanding your situation — what you're working on, what's working, what's not, and where you want to be. Then, if it sounds like I can help, I'll share a bit about how I'd approach it. And we'll leave time at the end to talk about next steps.
>
> Does that work for you?"

[They will almost always say yes. This sets the expectation that YOU are guiding the conversation.]

---

**Ask for recording permission (if applicable):**

> "One quick thing — would you mind if I record this call? It's just for my notes so I can refer back to exactly what we discussed. I won't share it with anyone."

---

## Part 2: Situation Questions (5-7 minutes)

**Goal:** Understand their current state — what their business looks like, what they've tried, and what they're working with. These are fact-finding questions.

---

> **"To start, can you give me the big picture of [THEIR COMPANY NAME]? What do you do, who do you serve, and where are things at right now?"**

[Let them talk. Take notes. This gives you context for everything that follows.]

---

> **"What does your [AREA RELATED TO YOUR SERVICE — e.g., 'marketing,' 'design process,' 'content strategy,' 'sales funnel'] look like today? Walk me through how it currently works."**

[You're mapping their current process so you can identify gaps and inefficiencies.]

---

> **"What have you tried so far to address [THE THING THEY REACHED OUT ABOUT]? What worked, what didn't?"**

[This tells you what approaches they've already exhausted, what they're skeptical of, and what they value.]

---

> **"Are you handling this in-house right now, or have you worked with someone else on it before?"**

[If they've worked with someone before, ask: "What went well with that experience? What would you want to be different this time?" This tells you their expectations and past frustrations.]

---

> **"How big is the team working on this? Who else is involved in this decision?"**

[This identifies stakeholders and helps you understand the decision-making process.]

---

## Part 3: Problem Questions (7-10 minutes)

**Goal:** Go deeper than the surface-level problem. Uncover the real pain — the frustration, the bottleneck, the thing that keeps them up at night. The deeper the pain, the higher the urgency, and the more they'll be willing to invest in a solution.

---

> **"What made you decide to look for help with this now? What changed?"**

[This reveals the trigger event. Something happened recently that turned a background annoyance into an active priority. Understand what that was.]

---

> **"If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about [THEIR PROBLEM AREA], what would it be?"**

[This cuts through complexity and reveals their top priority.]

---

> **"What's the biggest frustration you're dealing with in [SPECIFIC AREA]?"**

[Listen for emotional language — "It's killing us," "I'm sick of," "We keep losing." These phrases signal genuine pain.]

---

> **"What happens when [THE PROBLEM] doesn't get solved? What does that look like day-to-day?"**

[You're asking them to describe the ongoing consequences. This makes the pain tangible.]

---

> **"Is this problem getting worse, staying the same, or improving on its own?"**

[If it's getting worse, urgency increases. If it's staying the same, you may need to create urgency. If it's improving on its own, they may not need you.]

---

**When they mention something interesting, go deeper:**

> "Tell me more about that."
> "What do you mean by [SPECIFIC WORD THEY USED]?"
> "Can you give me an example?"
> "How long has that been going on?"

---

## Part 4: Impact Questions (5-7 minutes)

**Goal:** Quantify the cost of the problem. When the client understands how much the problem is costing them in dollars, time, or missed opportunities, the price of your solution becomes easy to justify.

---

> **"Do you have a sense of how much this is costing you — in revenue, time, or missed opportunities?"**

[Many clients haven't done this math. If they say "I'm not sure," help them calculate it:]

> "Let's think about it together. You mentioned [SPECIFIC DATA POINT — e.g., 'your conversion rate is at 2%' or 'you're losing 30% of users in the first week']. If you could move that to [REALISTIC IMPROVED NUMBER], what would that mean in terms of revenue?"

---

> **"How much time is your team spending on this each week? What else could they be doing with that time?"**

[Time cost is often overlooked. If a founder is spending 10 hours/week on something you can fix, that's 10 hours of their time that has a tangible dollar value.]

---

> **"What's the opportunity cost? What projects or initiatives are being delayed because this problem isn't solved?"**

[This reframes your service from a cost to an investment. You're not taking money from them — you're freeing them to pursue bigger opportunities.]

---

> **"If this problem were solved tomorrow, what would change for you personally?"**

[This question often reveals the emotional and personal stakes — less stress, better sleep, more confidence in the business, etc. These personal motivations are powerful.]

---

## Part 5: Vision Questions (3-5 minutes)

**Goal:** Get the client to describe their ideal outcome in their own words. You will use this language directly in your proposal to show that you understand what success looks like to them.

---

> **"Let's set the problem aside for a moment. If everything went perfectly over the next [3/6/12 MONTHS], what would [SPECIFIC AREA] look like?"**

[Let them paint the picture. Do not interrupt.]

---

> **"What would success look like for this specific project? How will you know it worked?"**

[This tells you their success metrics. Use these exact metrics in your proposal.]

---

> **"If we were sitting here [3/6 MONTHS] from now and this project was a home run, what would have happened?"**

[This is the "future pacing" question. It gets them emotionally invested in the outcome.]

---

## Part 6: Budget & Timeline Qualification (3-5 minutes)

**Goal:** Determine whether this prospect can afford you and whether the timeline is realistic. Many freelancers skip this section out of discomfort — do not skip it. Qualifying now saves you from writing proposals for people who can't afford you.

---

> **"What's driving the timeline on this? Is there a deadline or event you're working toward?"**

[External deadlines — a launch date, a board meeting, a conference, a funding milestone — create urgency and help you plan.]

---

> **"When would you ideally want to get started?"**

[If they say "yesterday," urgency is high. If they say "sometime in Q3," this may not be a hot lead.]

---

> **"Have you set aside a budget for this, or is that something you're still figuring out?"**

[This is the scariest question for most freelancers to ask. Ask it anyway. Here are ways to handle the common responses:]

**If they give you a range:**
> "That's helpful. I can work within that range. Let me put together a proposal with a few options and we'll find the right fit."

**If they say "I don't have a budget in mind":**
> "That's fine. Based on the scope of what we've discussed, projects like this typically fall in the range of $[LOW END] to $[HIGH END], depending on [VARIABLES — e.g., 'the number of pages,' 'the depth of the audit,' 'whether we're doing implementation or strategy only']. Does that range feel reasonable to you?"

**If they say "What do you charge?":**
> "It depends on the scope, which is why I wanted to understand your situation first. For a project like what you've described, I'd typically put together a proposal in the range of $[RANGE]. I'll include a few options at different levels so you can pick the one that fits."

**If the budget is clearly too low:**
> "I appreciate you being upfront about that. For the scope you've described, I wouldn't be able to do my best work within that range. What I could do is [REDUCED SCOPE OPTION — e.g., 'focus on the strategy and audit portion, and your team could handle implementation,' or 'tackle the highest-priority piece first and we can expand from there']. Would that be helpful?"

---

> **"Besides you, who else needs to sign off on this decision?"**

[If there are other decision-makers, ask: "Would it be helpful to include them on the next call, or would you prefer to review the proposal together with them internally?" Do not ignore this — proposals die in committee when you're not there to present them.]

---

## Part 7: Transition to Next Steps (3-5 minutes)

**Goal:** Summarize what you heard, confirm your understanding, and outline the next steps with a specific timeline.

---

**Summarize (this is where you demonstrate that you listened):**

> "Let me make sure I've got this right. [SUMMARIZE THEIR SITUATION, PROBLEM, AND GOALS IN 3-5 SENTENCES — e.g., 'You're currently at about 8,000 monthly visitors, but the conversion rate to trial signup is around 2%, which is well below where you'd like it. You've tried adjusting the copy internally, but it hasn't moved the needle. The main goal is to get to a 5% conversion rate by Q3, which would add roughly $[X] in monthly revenue.']. Did I miss anything?"

[Let them correct or add details. This step is crucial — it shows you listened and gives them confidence that you understand.]

---

**Signal your interest (if you want the project):**

> "I think this is a great fit for what I do. I've handled similar challenges with [BRIEF REFERENCE TO A RELEVANT PROJECT], and I have some ideas for how to approach this."

---

**Outline next steps with specific timelines:**

> "Here's what I'd suggest for next steps:
>
> 1. I'll put together a proposal with a few options based on everything we discussed today. I'll have that to you by [SPECIFIC DAY — ideally within 48 hours].
> 2. Once you've had a chance to review it, we can schedule a quick 15-minute call to walk through it and answer any questions.
> 3. If we decide to move forward, we can get started as soon as [DATE].
>
> Does that work for you?"

---

**End the call on a high note:**

> "This was a great conversation, [NAME]. I'm excited about the potential here and I'll get that proposal over to you by [DAY]. If anything comes to mind before then, feel free to email me."

## Objection Handling Scripts

These objections typically come up at the end of the discovery call or during a follow-up conversation about the proposal. Do not panic. Objections mean they are considering working with you — they just need help getting past a concern.

---

### "It's too expensive."

**Do not immediately drop your price.** Instead, reframe the cost against the cost of the problem.

> "I understand — it's a significant investment. Can I ask you something? Earlier you mentioned that [THE PROBLEM] is costing you roughly [QUANTIFIED IMPACT — e.g., '$15,000/month in lost conversions,' '20 hours of your team's time per week']. If this project solves that, the return on investment would be [X] within the first [TIMEFRAME].
>
> That said, I want this to work for your budget. I can also put together a smaller-scope option that focuses on [HIGHEST-PRIORITY PIECE] so we can start with the biggest lever and expand from there. Would that be worth exploring?"

---

### "I need to think about it."

**Do not say "Sure, take your time." Instead, uncover what's really going on.**

> "Of course — I wouldn't want you to rush into anything. Can I ask what's giving you pause? Is it the budget, the timing, the scope, or something else?
>
> I ask because sometimes I can adjust the proposal to address whatever's on your mind, and I'd rather do that now than leave you wondering."

[If they genuinely need time:]

> "Completely understand. How about this — I'll follow up with you on [SPECIFIC DAY, 3-5 days out]. Does that give you enough time to think it over? And if any questions come up before then, feel free to reach out anytime."

---

### "We already have someone / we handle this in-house."

**Do not compete on features. Compete on outcomes.**

> "That makes sense. Can I ask how that's been going? Are you seeing the results you're hoping for?
>
> [If they express any dissatisfaction:] That's actually pretty common. What I've found is that [YOUR DIFFERENTIATION — e.g., 'an outside perspective can often unlock improvements that are hard to see when you're inside the business,' 'specialized expertise in this specific area can compress timelines significantly,' 'having a dedicated resource focused only on this, rather than splitting attention across multiple responsibilities, tends to produce better results'].
>
> I'm not suggesting you replace what's working. But would it be valuable to have a second opinion? I'm happy to do a quick audit at no cost so you can see how your current approach compares to what I'd recommend."

---

### "Can you just send a proposal?"

**This usually means they want to skip the discovery call (if asked before the call) or cut the current call short. Do not just send a proposal without qualifying them — you'll waste hours on a document they may never read.**

If they ask **before** the discovery call:

> "I'd love to, and I will — but I've found that proposals I send without understanding your specific situation end up being generic and not very useful. If we spend 20-25 minutes on a call, I can put together something tailored to exactly what [THEIR COMPANY NAME] needs, with realistic pricing and timelines. Would [DATE/TIME] work for a quick chat?"

If they ask **during** the call to wrap up early:

> "Absolutely, I'll get that over to you. Just a couple more quick questions so I can make the proposal as relevant as possible — [ASK YOUR 2-3 MOST IMPORTANT REMAINING QUESTIONS]. That'll help me give you accurate pricing and a realistic scope."

## Post-Call Action Items Checklist

Complete these within 2 hours of the call while everything is fresh.

- [ ] **Send a thank-you email** within 1 hour. Keep it short: "Hi [NAME], thanks for the great conversation today. I'm excited about the opportunity and I'll have the proposal to you by [DAY]. In the meantime, feel free to reach out if anything comes to mind."
- [ ] **Transcribe or review your notes.** If you recorded the call, skim the transcript and highlight key phrases and data points. If you took manual notes, clean them up and fill in any gaps.
- [ ] **Log the prospect in your pipeline tracker.** Move them from "Call Scheduled" to "Call Complete" and note the next action (proposal due date).
- [ ] **Identify the 3 most important pain points** from the call. These will anchor your proposal's Problem Statement.
- [ ] **Note their exact words** for describing the problem and the desired outcome. Use this language in the proposal — it makes them feel heard.
- [ ] **Draft the proposal.** Use the Proposal Template bonus and the AI prompt at the end of it to generate a first draft from your call notes. Edit, personalize, and send within 48 hours.
- [ ] **Set a follow-up reminder.** If you haven't heard back within 3 business days of sending the proposal, follow up with a brief check-in email.
- [ ] **Evaluate the lead.** Ask yourself: Is this a client I genuinely want to work with? Do they have the budget? Is the timeline realistic? Is the project within my expertise? If the answer to any of these is no, it's okay to pass.
