## Chapter 8: The Discovery Call Framework

A lead responded to your outreach. They're interested. Now what?

The discovery call is where leads become clients — or where they disappear forever. Most freelancers wing these calls, talking too much about themselves and too little about the prospect's actual problem. The result: low close rates, "I'll think about it" responses, and proposals that go nowhere.

This chapter gives you a complete, repeatable framework for discovery calls that convert.

### Pre-Call Preparation

Never go into a discovery call cold. Spend 10-15 minutes preparing — AI makes this fast.

**Pre-call research prompt:**

```
I have a discovery call with a potential client. Here's what I know:

Name: [name]
Title: [title]
Company: [company name]
Website: [URL]
What they do: [brief description]
How they found me: [cold email response, referral, inbound, etc.]
What they said when booking: [any context from their email/message]

Prepare a discovery call brief:
1. Company overview: What do they do, who are their customers, what's their business model?
2. Potential pain points related to [your service area]
3. 3 specific observations about their [website/marketing/product] I can reference in the call
4. 5 questions tailored to their situation that will uncover their real needs
5. What a successful engagement might look like for them
6. Any potential red flags to watch for
```

**Pre-call checklist:**
- [ ] Researched their company and website
- [ ] Reviewed the email thread that led to this call
- [ ] Prepared 5 tailored questions
- [ ] Have a quiet environment with good audio
- [ ] Calendar open for scheduling next steps

### The 5-Part Discovery Call Script

**Total call time**: 30-45 minutes

**Part 1: Rapport and Agenda (3-5 minutes)**

Start by taking control of the call — in a friendly way. This sets the tone.

> "Thanks for making time, [Name]. Before we dive in, let me suggest a quick agenda so we use this time well. I'd love to learn about [company] and what you're working on, understand where [your service area] fits into your goals, and then if it makes sense, we can talk about what working together might look like. Sound good?"

This does three things: it shows professionalism, it gives the prospect a roadmap, and it positions you as a consultant (not a vendor begging for work).

**Part 2: Situation Questions (5-10 minutes)**

Understand their current state before you dig into problems.

Key questions:
- "Tell me about [company] — what's the big focus for you right now?"
- "How does [your service area, e.g., 'your website/marketing/content'] fit into your growth plans?"
- "What's working well right now? What are you happy with?"
- "What does your current setup look like for [your service area]?"
- "Who's involved in these decisions on your end?"

**Listen more than you talk.** Your job in this section is to understand their world, not pitch your solution.

**Part 3: Problem Questions (10-15 minutes)**

This is the most important part. Uncover the specific pain that will drive them to hire you.

Key questions:
- "What's the biggest challenge you're facing with [service area] right now?"
- "How long has this been a problem? What have you tried so far?"
- "What's preventing you from solving this internally?"
- "If you could fix one thing about [specific area], what would it be?"
- "What happens if you don't address this? What's the cost of doing nothing?"

**The "cost of doing nothing" question is gold.** It forces the prospect to quantify their pain — which becomes the justification for your fee.

For example: "We're spending $15K/month on ads driving traffic to a website that converts at 1%. If we could get that to 3%, that's like tripling our ad spend efficiency." Now your $10K redesign proposal looks like a bargain.

**Part 4: Impact and Vision (5-10 minutes)**

Help them see the future state — what solving this problem would mean.

Key questions:
- "If we solved this, what would that mean for [company/team/you personally]?"
- "What does success look like? How would you measure it?"
- "What timeline are you working with? Any deadlines driving this?"
- "Is there a budget allocated for this, or would we need to build a case for investment?"

**Don't skip the budget question.** Many freelancers are afraid to ask about budget because it feels "salesy." But knowing the budget early prevents you from spending hours on a proposal that's 5x their range.

**How to ask about budget naturally:**
> "To make sure I scope this appropriately — do you have a budget range in mind? Even a ballpark helps me tailor the proposal to what makes sense for you."

If they push back: "I don't want to anchor too high or too low. If you can share a range, I can show you what's achievable within it."

**Part 5: Next Steps (3-5 minutes)**

Always end with a clear next step. Never end with "I'll send you something."

> "Based on everything we've discussed, I think there's a great fit here. Here's what I'd suggest as next steps: I'll put together a proposal that covers [summarize their problem and your approach] with a few options for scope and investment. I'll have that to you by [specific date — within 3-5 business days]. Then we can review it together on a quick call. Does that work?"

Key principles:
- **Set a deadline for yourself** — a specific date for the proposal
- **Propose a review call** — don't just email the proposal into the void
- **Summarize what you heard** — this proves you listened and understood

### Handling Common Objections

**"We need to think about it"**
> "Completely understand. It's a significant decision. Can I ask — what specifically would you want to think through? Sometimes I can provide additional information that helps."

Then address whatever they raise. If they're vague, offer: "How about I send over the proposal, and we schedule a 15-minute call next week to discuss any questions?"

**"Your price is too high"**
> "I hear you. Let me ask — is it the total investment, or is the timing an issue? Because we could look at phasing this into stages that spread the cost out."

Alternatively: "What were you expecting? I can adjust the scope to work within a different budget, though the results would be proportionally different."

**"We already have someone"**
> "That makes sense. What made you take this call — is there something your current [designer/writer/etc.] isn't covering, or are you looking for a second perspective?"

**"Can you just send me a proposal?"**
> "Absolutely. To make sure the proposal actually addresses your real needs, can I ask a few more questions? That way you get something specific and useful, not a generic pitch."

(Then continue the discovery call — they're trying to skip the conversation, but you need the information to write a proposal that converts.)

### Post-Call Action Items

Immediately after the call:

```
Here are my notes from a discovery call with a potential client:

[Paste your raw notes]

Create a structured summary:
1. Company overview and current situation
2. Key problems identified (ranked by urgency)
3. Budget and timeline
4. Decision-making process (who else is involved?)
5. What they're looking for in a solution
6. Key things they said in their own words (for the proposal)
7. Red flags or concerns
8. Recommended next steps and proposal approach
```

This becomes the foundation for your proposal. See the **Discovery Call Script** bonus for the complete printable version.

## Chapter 9: AI-Powered Proposal Writing System

The proposal is where deals are won or lost. A great discovery call means nothing if your proposal is generic, confusing, or fails to connect your solution to their specific problem.

Most freelancer proposals lose because they're about the freelancer, not the client. They list capabilities, show portfolio pieces, and quote a price. They read like a resume, not a solution.

### The Winning Proposal Structure

Your proposal should follow the exact journey of the prospect's thinking:

**1. Executive Summary (half a page)**
Restate their problem and your solution in their own words. This proves you listened. The prospect should read this and think: "Yes, that's exactly our situation."

**2. The Problem (1 page)**
Describe their current state, the specific challenges, and the cost of not solving them. Use their own language from the discovery call. Quantify the impact: "With your current 1.2% conversion rate and $15K monthly ad spend, approximately $12,500/month in potential revenue is being left on the table."

**3. The Solution (1-2 pages)**
Describe your approach — not as a list of tasks, but as a journey toward their goal. Focus on *what they get*, not *what you do*. Include milestones so they can see the path clearly.

**4. Proof (half a page)**
One or two relevant case studies. Not your full portfolio — just the most relevant results for a client like them. "We helped [similar company] in a similar situation, resulting in [specific outcome]."

**5. Investment (1 page)**
Use the 3-tier pricing strategy (see below). Present the price as an investment with clear ROI framing.

**6. Next Steps (half a page)**
Tell them exactly what happens if they say yes. "Sign below, and here's what Week 1 looks like."

### The 3-Tier Pricing Strategy

Never offer one price. Offer three options:

| | Essentials | Growth | Premium |
|---|---|---|---|
| Scope | Core deliverables only | Core + extras | Everything + premium additions |
| Timeline | Standard | Standard | Accelerated |
| Support | Email only | Email + 2 check-ins | Dedicated weekly calls |
| **Price** | $X | $X × 1.5-1.8 | $X × 2.2-2.8 |

Why this works:
- **Anchoring**: The Premium tier makes the Growth tier feel reasonable
- **Choice**: The prospect chooses *which* option, not *whether* to hire you
- **Upsell**: 30-40% of clients choose the middle or top tier
- **Flexibility**: Price-sensitive clients can choose Essentials without you lowering your rate

### AI-Powered Proposal Generation

After your discovery call, use this prompt to generate a first draft:

```
I need to write a project proposal for a potential client. Here are my discovery call notes:

[Paste your call summary from the post-call AI prompt]

My service: [your service]
My positioning: [your positioning statement]

Write a complete proposal with these sections:
1. Executive Summary — restate their problem and my solution in THEIR language
2. The Challenge — describe their current situation and the cost of inaction (use specific numbers from the call if available)
3. Proposed Solution — describe my approach as a journey, not a task list. Include clear milestones.
4. Relevant Experience — write a brief case study of [describe a similar past project and its results]
5. Investment — present three tiers (Essentials, Growth, Premium) with the base price of [your price]. Describe what each tier includes and why they might choose it.
6. Timeline — projected timeline for each tier
7. Next Steps — what happens when they sign

Requirements:
- Focus on THEIR problem and outcome, not my credentials
- Use their own words where possible
- Keep it under 4 pages
- Sound confident and professional, not salesy
- Include specific numbers and results where possible
```

Review the draft, customize with details only you know, and send. Total time: 30-45 minutes for a complete, compelling proposal.

### When to Send

- **Within 3-5 business days** of the discovery call (any longer and momentum dies)
- **Tuesday-Thursday** for highest open rates
- **Morning** in their timezone
- **With a note**: Don't just attach and send. Write a brief email summarizing the call, express excitement about the opportunity, and suggest a time to walk through the proposal together.

> "Hi [Name], great speaking with you on [day]. As promised, here's a proposal addressing [their core problem] with a few options for scope and investment. I'd love to walk you through it — would [specific day/time] work for a quick 20-minute call?"

Always propose a review call. Proposals discussed live close at 2-3x the rate of proposals sent and forgotten.

## Chapter 10: Follow-Up Sequences That Close

Here's a stat that should change how you think about sales:

- **48% of freelancers never follow up** after sending a proposal
- **80% of deals close after the 5th follow-up**
- **Most freelancers give up after 1-2 follow-ups**

The gap between "sent a proposal" and "closed the deal" is follow-up. It's not talent, pricing, or portfolio quality. It's persistence — done professionally.

### The 7-Touch Follow-Up Sequence

After sending a proposal, here's your follow-up cadence:

**Touch 1: Proposal sent (Day 0)**
The proposal itself, with the note suggesting a review call.

**Touch 2: Gentle check-in (Day 3)**

```
Subject: Re: Proposal for [Company Name]

Hi [Name],

Just wanted to make sure the proposal came through okay. I know inboxes can be unpredictable.

I'm available [specific times] this week if you'd like to walk through it together. Sometimes it's easier to discuss the options live.

Let me know!

[Your name]
```

**Touch 3: Add value (Day 7)**

```
Subject: Re: Proposal for [Company Name]

Hi [Name],

While putting together your proposal, I noticed [specific observation about their business — something helpful, not a criticism]. I thought this might be useful regardless of whether we work together: [brief, actionable insight or tip].

Let me know if you have any questions about the proposal. Happy to adjust anything based on your needs.

[Your name]
```

**Touch 4: Social proof (Day 12)**

```
Subject: Re: Proposal for [Company Name]

Hi [Name],

Quick update — I just wrapped up a project with [similar company type] where we [specific result]. Reminded me of our conversation about [their specific challenge].

If you're still evaluating options, I'm happy to share more details about that project. And of course, if you have questions about the proposal, I'm here.

[Your name]
```

**Touch 5: Direct check-in (Day 18)**

```
Subject: Re: Proposal for [Company Name]

Hi [Name],

I wanted to check in on the [project type] proposal. Are you still considering moving forward, or has the timeline shifted?

If priorities have changed, totally understand. If you're still interested but the proposal needs adjusting — different scope, different budget, different timing — I'm very open to that conversation.

What's the best next step?

[Your name]
```

**Touch 6: The pivot (Day 25)**

```
Subject: [Company Name] — quick question

Hi [Name],

I know timing doesn't always align, and I respect that. Quick question: is this project still on your radar for this quarter, or is it something you're planning for later in the year?

If later, I'm happy to circle back at a better time. Just let me know and I'll add a reminder.

[Your name]
```

**Touch 7: The breakup (Day 35)**

```
Subject: Should I close your file?

Hi [Name],

I haven't heard back, so I'll assume the timing isn't right for [project type] right now. Totally respect that.

I'm going to close out this opportunity on my end, but the door is always open. If [specific problem you discussed] becomes a priority again, just reply to this email and we'll pick up where we left off.

Wishing you and [company] all the best.

[Your name]
```

The breakup email has the highest response rate of the entire sequence — typically 25-40%. People respond because they feel the window closing, or because they feel guilty about ghosting, or because the timing actually has changed and this gives them an easy way to re-engage.

### How to Follow Up Without Being Annoying

Rules:
1. **Every follow-up adds value** — new insight, social proof, helpful observation. Never "just bumping this."
2. **Respect their intelligence** — don't pretend you're emailing about something else. Be direct.
3. **Give them an easy out** — "If the timing has changed, no worries" removes pressure and paradoxically makes them more likely to engage.
4. **Space them appropriately** — the cadence above is designed to be persistent without being oppressive.

### When to Walk Away

Not every lead will close. Know when to stop:
- After 7 follow-ups with no response: close the file. Move on.
- If they explicitly say no: thank them, ask for feedback, move on.
- If they say "not now": ask when to follow up, set a calendar reminder, and genuinely follow up then.
- If the red flags from your discovery call are piling up: trust your gut. Bad clients cost more than no clients.

Log every lost deal and the reason in your pipeline tracker. Over time, patterns emerge: maybe you're losing deals on price (adjust positioning or targeting), or on timing (improve your trigger-event prospecting), or on scope (adjust your discovery call questions).
